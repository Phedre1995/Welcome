Positions of a Noun in a Sentence

A noun can hold the following positions in a sentence:
Subject, direct object, indirect object, subject complement, object complement, object of a preposition, appositive, adjective.

Noun as the Subject

A noun can hold the subject position of a sentence.
E.g

  1. Bianca recently had a birthday.
  2. Cars can modulate their speed.
In the first sentence, Bianca holds the subject position and had is the corresponding verb. What type of noun is this? In the second sentence, the subject is the noun cars. Modulate is the corresponding verb.


Noun as the Object

Nouns can hold the object position of a sentence. Either direct object or indirect object.
  1. E.g. As a direct object: Shut down your computer before bed.
  2. E.g. As an indirect object:Shut down your computer before bed

Noun as the Subject Complement

E.g.Humans are humans.
In the above example, humans acts as the subject complement. Nouns as subject complements usually follow linking verbs such as to be, to seem, to become.

Noun as the Object Complement

E.g.Apple trees provide cool shade on sunny days..
In the above example, the nominal (noun) phrase on sunny days acts as the object complement. It describes the direct object of the sentence.

Noun as the Object of a Preposition

E.g.Apple trees provide cool shade on sunny days..
In the above example, the nominal (noun) phrase on sunny days acts as the object complement, and particularly the object of the preposition on.

Noun as the Appositive

Appositive nouns are nouns that immediately follow another noun to further describe or define it.
E.g.Jessica is twelve years old.; Helen is a grease monkey.; This business is a cash cow.

Noun as the Adjective

E.g.A love story.;A pride parade.; A foot stool.;
The emboldened nouns act as modifiers and adjectives of the nouns they follow them. And vice versa.

Possessive Nouns

In order to create the possessive form of noun, you need to mark the possessor with an apostrophe and an s. E.g.The car's windshield.; My shirt's collar.; This house is mine. It is yours.
Mine is an exception to the rule of "s" with possessiveness.

Plural Nouns

In order to create the possessive form of nouns, unlike collective nouns, you need to conjugate it to a plural verb, and end the word with an "s"or "es". Although there are many exceptions to this rule. E.g.The cars are in the lot. (car); Butterflies scare me. (butterflies); Oxen are bred in farms.(Ox);
The plural nouns are conjugated with a plural verb. Whereas a collective noun (e.g. Troop) is conjugated with a singular verb (see KASSCON LESSON 3).

Countable Nouns and Uncountable Nouns

Much like the name describes, there exist countable nouns which can easily be counted, and uncountbale which are too difficult to keep track of.
Countable nouns can be measured by the following articles: a/an, the, many, a few, a couple, some, many.
On the other hand, uncountable nouns are too difficult to count, and can be measured with: a lot of, a little, much, some, any.
E.g.A little food; Five cars; a lot of milk; There aren't any shoes; There isn't any rice.
Uncountable nouns take a singular verb, because they are seen as one entity.
